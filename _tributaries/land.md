GLEN CANYON IS a landscape in constant motion. Here, the contours of space rarely hold still: bits of earth--sediment, rock dust, sandstone shards--are at all times sloughing away from their bedrock encasings, etched out by wind, water, animal hoof, or plant root. 

Some of these loose bits are plucked up by wind, and set down in the crevices of the canyon's rilled buttes, hills, and cliffs; others pile up into high dunes. Some bits are taken up by water--carried by a trickle of rain or the Colorado River itself--to heap up on sandy shore, or to lodge along the river's bed. And so the edges of things waver and drift, and the ground swells and subsides, like a viscous, slow-moving sea. 

This is not *terra firma,* solid earth, fixed space--this is a terrain always in flux: fissures, cut by flowing water, worm through the soft sandstone ground. Eventually, cracks grow into chasms and splinter into branches. For as long as these canyons exist, they will meander and writhe like great sluggish snakes. 

Of course, all landscapes shift over time: elementary-aged children can tell you that mountains rise and crumble, that rivers carve valleys and deep canyons, and the tectonic plates will eventually tear the continents apart and bring new ones together. But barring natural catastrophe--earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, landslides--these mutations have, for centuries, been imagined to take place at such a gradual, creeping pace they are nearly imperceptible, and therefore inconsequential, for human life.

We have come to expect, in other words, that the ground will remain firm beneath our feet. 

However, not all humans view nature as so inert and inanimate: author Amitav Ghosh reminds us this is a Western way of thinking, and even then, gradualist ideas only gained prevalence at the turn of the 19th century. It was at this point that geologists James Hutton and Charles Lyell began propounding ideas that the earth was formed not through a series of violent calamities, but by incremental accruals, erosions, and shifts. The theory stuck, partially because Lyell slandered his detractors as primitive and guilty of mystical thinking. Those who believed in a more capricious earth, Lyell said, were effectively ascribing natural processes "to the intervention of demons, ghosts, witches, and other immaterial and supernatural agents" (Ghosh 27). But Lyell's theories also gained sway when Darwin took up the logics of gradualism, and applied them to his model of evolution, and as Ghosh elucidates, Westerners have since imagined that, for the most part, "Nature does not make leaps" (26). 

Thus, in the Western eye, nature has rendered nature functionally static: hills and mountains certainly drift, gradualists say, but they do so at a pace that has no bearing on human activity. A family who builds a house upon a hill need not worry about the hill blowing away (even the earth-shaping potential of natural disasters were downplayed by gradualist doctrines). And so nature was imagined as a passive stage upon which human dramas takes place (78). 

But nature is not inert, even at the human scale. In Glen Canyon, one does not have to stand in place for a thousand years to witness the drifting of the hills: I have seen the landscape morph with my own eyes, and many others have seen this too.

## NatureisFuckingLit

Last winter, I visited Lone Rock, a solitary butte that rises high out of Wahweap Bay, one of Lake Powell's coves. Decades ago, when the lake was at full pool, Lone Rock was encircled by deep water, but the lake has long since retreated, leaving behind a vast bed of sand and a shallow, mucky puddle. Even as I walked across the sand flats, I could see that beach had grown longer and the lake even smaller since I had last been to Lone Rock four years before. With the lake shrunk down, Lone Rock seemed to become taller, its bleached bathtub rings wider. Over a period so short I could not have pointed to any measurable change in the composition of my own body, the contours of the land had been remade, though in this case by shifting water, not shifting earth.

In other places, the earth's contours are maintained by water, and are remade in its absence. Two days before I went to Lone Rock, I took a walking tour of Antelope Canyon, whose pink swerving walls you might recognize from many a stock computer desktop background. Deep in the canyon, our guide pointed a rock that took the shape of George Washington's profile, and then she pulled out her phone to show the group a photograph of the same place, five years earlier.

Do you see, she asked us, that the floor is ten feet higher now than it was then? One by one, we looked at her screen, and then back up at George Washington, trying to reconcile the two different places that were actually the same. Now, we could reach up and touch George Washington's nose. Then, Washington's chin floated high above the canyon floor. Because of the drought,she explained, the spring floods had not come, and so the sand that the wind steadily sifts into the canyon during the winter and autumn had not been flushed as usual. Instead, sand was filling up the canyon floor. The man next to me, a former pharmaceutical executive from Israel, looked down at the ground with an air of surprise, perhaps trying to imagine, as I was, how the coordinates of space could have be rearranged so quickly, how a floor could appear where there used to be only air. 

<br><br><br>

Sometimes, water and earth come together and remake the land into drastic and sudden new forms. In the mere sixty years since the Glen Canyon Dam's construction, the Colorado River has built towering mud glaciers--some two hundred feet tall--at the point where flowing river meets still lake (this point itself is always moving, according to the lake's level at any given time. A fuller lake means the delta point lies further upstream, a lower lake means the delta will move downstream). Lake Powell's mud glaciers, which are still new and uncompacted by the forces of time, are loosely bound, and therefore prone to collapse. Boaters have captured videos of great hunks of mud calving off their banks and slumping into the river. When these mud bergs fall, they rearrange the river into new channels, and they whip up rapids that shift day by day. 

A friend of mine, who runs raft trips on the Grand Canyon all summer long, recently described to me a similar phenemona occuring where the Grand Canyon begins to empty into Lake Mead. She said that nobody, even those guides who had worked the Canyon for decades, knew anymore what they would find around the Canyon's final bends. Garganutuan standing waves piling up over underwater mud bergs? A river split into three or four or five narrow channels, or squeezed into one torrential stream? The ground was shifting too rapidly for them to keep up. 

On a reddit page--one of my favorites--r/NatureisFuckingLit--I recently came across a video of one such "strange phenomena" at Lake Powell. The video was titled "Underwater Avalanche?!" and subtitled, more scientifically, "Turbidity current at Lake Powell." In the footage, a cloud of murky, seething water spreads out across the lake's otherwise blue and placid surface--it looks as if the lake has begun to boil. Suddenly, the cloud darts downstream, trailing a churning path, and I cannot help but think of the way the sandworms in *Dune* push up ridges of slithering sand before they breach. 

{% include feature/video.html objectid="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ltshXuLuwJM" width="100" %}

Then the cameraman zooms in on a dark object slipping quickly through the now whirling water, and he wonders out loud if it's animal trapped in the current--before realizing, with even greater alarm, it's in fact an entire log being pulled down into the lake. He pans to his boat's depth gauge, and remarks that the gauge is acting strange: "it reads almost 150 feet" he says, although "it should be less than 20." Perhaps, he jokes, he has just seen the Lake Powell monster. 

For me and the cameraman alike, the river's surprising behavior conjures up a supernatural image: only something alive, our instincts both said, could cause the earth to move with such erratic agency. Like Charles Lyell's "primitive" thinkers, both of us turned towards the realm of demons, ghosts and witches to explain what we saw. But it strikes me that there is some irony here. When those of us still inculcated in Lyell's gradualist paradigms encounter an natural that is not gradual at all, we are left with few ways to process what it is we are seeing. With no "natural" explanation at hand, we are drawn back out into the "supernatural," a word that of course only means that which is beyond whatever delineation of *natural* is available. 

Even as I can cognitively critique gradualist constructs, for me, there *is* something unsettling in recognizing that nature is not so orderly or predictable after all. I cannot help but feel a prickle of squeamish distress when I think about the ground beneath me not as still but as actually *in motion*, like I'm standing over a pit of squirming, wriggling snakes. And I am even more unsettled knowing that even a highly-managed, manmade lake can suddenly open up and swallow logs whole.

Ghosh groups these feelings of disoriented, unsettled, squeamish distress into the category of uncanniness. The uncanny, he says, emerges in "those moments when something that seems inanimate turns out to be vitally, even dangerously alive" (3). Recognition of the lively earth means recognizing that even in those moments in which we think we are alone, there are in fact innumerable things in motion all around us, things that might catch us up and bog us down in their flows. 

{% include feature/image-gallery.html objectid="sediment5" caption=false%}

But I do not think that encounters with a more vital earth are always so horror-inducing. When elementary children learn about plate tectonics--or when the man in Antelope Canyon realized he was standing on a moving floor--or when my friend told me about the Grand Canyon generating a new and different riverscape every day, like some fervent artist--there is also awe, excitement, and captivation. 

Perhaps this is why I like to scroll through r/NatureisFuckingLit. It feels good to look through the kaleidoscope of the earth's expressions: curtains of rain moving over a city like an army of ghosts; glittering red sand swirling in the ocean's tides; winds whipping snow into snaking ropes on a frozen lake; baby woodcocks luring worms out of the ground with a tiny, bobbing dance; sparrow murmurations rippling across the sky--and to be reminded of the surprising creativity, multiplicity, and vitality of our planet. r/NatureisFuckingLit is far from a space of existential horror and distress. By its own description, its 1.2 million subscribers are "here to appreciate the awesome majesty and incredibly cool aspects of nature 🔥." This subreddit, seems to me, to signify a collective impulse to witness and catalog forms of agency that are explicitly *not* human. 

Perhaps this impulse is driven by a desire to know we humans are not alone, that we are not drifting through space on some cold chunk of rock, disconnected from everything and everyone but ourselves. I also sense a current of pride in the subreddit, as if we humans are also implicated by the bright mosaics of our planet's expressions. Perhaps, after uncanniness, there is something else we might find in recognizing that we are nestled into the vibrant communities of others who are here with us.

## Sedimentation

{% include feature/image-gallery.html objectid="sediment179" caption=false%}

But what of the canyon walls, which do not suddenly warp and and twist, but garner so much awe precisely because they seem to have stood exactly in place for millions and billions of years? Many who have visited the Canyons of the Colorado River, in fact, are struck not by the land's motion, but by its remarkable stillness. Author Kevin Fedarko, who in 2015 accomplished the impressive feat of walking through length of the Grand Canyon, writes of "an absolute dearth of sound or movement." And Colin Fletcher, who completed the same journey in 1963, spoke of "silence so profound that the whole colossal chaos of rock and space and color seemed to have sunk beneath it and to lie there cut off, timeless." (6). 

To descend into the Colorado River's canyons is to descend into a place where time and space have ossified. If you were travel to the innermost gorge of the Grand Canyon, where the Canyon plunges more than a mile below its rim, you would find--could even reach out and touch--the very basement layers of the earth's rocks. Here, the river has exposed polished black slabs of Vishnu Schist that have held in place for nearly two billion years--half the age of the earth itself, and more than a tenth the age of the universe. 

{% include feature/image-gallery.html objectid="sediment335" caption=false%}

Some parts of the earth are riotous with motion, but sometimes the earth is also very, very still. 

And so Lyell and Hutton were not all wrong: some landscapes *do* move at a pace so slow, their movement evades the limits of human perception. Of course, most things in the universe, the vast majority, are not calibrated to fit conveniently within the confines of human senses. Technology has helped us to augment our perceptive reach, but we can still only register what few phenomena happen to fall within our narrow frames. And so we humans, naturally, tend to condense the differentiated rhythms of time and space into a kind of moderate uniformity. 

The earth, however, can move both extremely fast and extremely slow, and every pace in between; there is no one uniform pace. Rocks can stand still for billions of years, and volcanoes can blow mountain tops off in a moment. This might all seem obvious to say, but it gestures to a fundamental flaw with Lyell and Hutton's theories: that the earth's processes might be explained through one all-encompassing law, through a geologic theory of everything. 

{% include feature/image-gallery.html objectid="sediment30" caption=false%}

No such homogeneity exists in nature, and it seems to me there is very little chance of understanding much at all through sweeping universalisms, as when reductionist theories gain absolute sway, other truths may become eclipsed and obscured. 

For instance: geologic strata tell one history of the earth: one of slow, incremental change over immense spans of time. This is a true story, but it is not the only story. By their very nature, geologic formations are sites where movement has been compressed out, where things are cemented in place. Objects in flux, by definition, cannot be captured in this format, and so the geologic record is a catalog of things that have stopped moving.

What does not make it in to this record are the bits of the earth that are still in motion: the rock flecks that still fly around on the wind, bone dust still flowing the world's rivers and oceans, and the literal quintillians of animals and plants still moving on their own volitions,  whose flesh will likely never be preserved in the geologic archive.   

And so when geologic strata become the axiom of historical knowledge, the undisputed record of the past, the world in motion is left out. This is an omission of extreme proportions, not only because so much of the world's stuff is *not* petrified in stone, but because the reality we really live in is precisely one of constant movement, in which nothing ever stands perfectly still. 

{% include feature/image-gallery.html objectid="sediment209" caption=false%}

Yet a history without motion renders space and time down a series of "static slices," as if reality is drawn up like a cartoon in which the characters are not actually moving, but the illusion of movement is instead created by stringing together a series of still frames. However, as geographer Doreen Massey reminds us, movement "cannot be broken up into discrete instants" and imagining history as a sequence of stacked layers fails to "bring alive the trajectories" which in fact "co-form" space. (110). "Static time-slices" she writes, "even multiplied to infinity, cannot produce becoming" (23). Nor can geologic strata, no matter how deep or finely layered produce becoming either. 

There is another problem with trying to understand the world without motion: when things are in motion, they come into contact with each other, or tear apart, and thus create a sprawling and constantly shifting network of connections and disconnections. In Massey's eyes, it is this network of interrelations and interactions that creates space itself, which she argues is not then separate from time. Space, for Massey, is movement, or in her own words, it is the "dimension of multiple trajectories, a simultaneity of stories-so-far" (24). 

There is a political edge to whether we represent space as a fixed and layered plane or as a rhizome of criss-crossing trajectories. Massey writes that "the notion of a section in which all elements exist in immediate relationship with one another is essentially a description of a closed system" which in turn "robs it of all political potential." Imagining space as fixed, in other words, shutters the possibility for new relationships to form, and therefore shutters the possibility for social arrangements to be reformulated into new political structures. 

This edge is double-sided, because stabilizing space not only forecloses certain futures, but can also serve to erase certain pasts. Massey argues that practices which lay out space on fixed surfaces--such as mapping--make relational trajectories invisible. "It is thus, she concludes, "that others are rendered static, without history" (121), and space becomes a "blank page" to be explored. 

{% include feature/image-gallery.html objectid="sediment29" caption=false%}


Like Massey, I am interested pressing against forms of representation that distill space into static layers and that exclude motion. While Massey is most concerned with the stabilizing power of maps, I am concerned with the stabilizing power of strata, and I am wary of thinking that enlists strata as the primary archive of the earth's history. Yet I do not wish to make my own universalisms here: I want to simultaneously accommodate the heterogeneity of spatial motions and immotions; I want to include the blowing rock flecks, the flowing bone dust, as well as the very still and very old canyon walls. 

Perhaps you can see where this is going: I am advocating for a sedimentary model of space, for sediment can at once sit encased--sedimented, in fact--in bedrock for billions of years, but can also flow out of that bedrock as it is cut out by water, wind,  plant root, or animal foot. Sediment reveals an earth that is very old and still and at the same time, is in motion and in process. But this earth is always in relation, and it is always unfinished, even if its arrangements and derangements occur along widely differentiated rhythms. This sedimentary model can work both literally and metaphorically; I am interested in following sediment through Glen Canyon's history because sediment helps me to trace out its sprawling webs of shifting relations. 

A mud glacier at once encodes, for example, how water, earth, and infrastructure come together to produce new and strange landscapes, in which some forms of life might be buried and pushed out, and others might find fertile ground to root into. And if that glacier is laced through with the refuse of spilled mines or agricultural waste, as many of them are, I am able to track those sites where the spoils of bygone economies or far away industries lie latent in the earth, threatening to disperse across the atmosphere if they meet the right combination of climate-induced drought and wind.

## Mud + Failure

Yet mud resists the lure of endless forward
momentum. The lure of endless forward momentum comes in many guises,
as Arturo has shown: development, sustainability, modernity, globalization,
to name a few. But the muddy earth, literally and figuratively, slows things
down. Mud is antimodernity. Mud holds place and territory dear. Mud is the
earth’s version of resistance. Mud archives overlooked histories.
Mud is a life project, too. Challenging our basic understandings of what
constitutes living, mud teems with sulfuric, anaerobic life. Bacteria, crayfish,
worms, and the like flourish in the muddy earth. Tiny creatures create sub-
terranean burrows in the mud, a meshwork of sorts, which enable oxygen
and nutrients to cycle across multiple strata. In its solid form, mud creates
architectures of life and loss, from sunbaked adobe walls to funereal urns.
Muddy floors are a vexing reminder of the futility of keeping nature out.
Muddiness by definition is an ontological mess: land or sea, earth or water,
nature or culture?
Yet this ontological shiftiness enables the muddy earth’s radical potential.
Mud could be mistaken as life at the slow margins of the world (particularly
by those entranced by the lure of endless forward momentum). (45)

## Earthly Archives

AT THE END of 2025, I visited Lake Powell. The day I arrived, a historic windstorm was sweeping across the West, buffeting back my car with sixty-mile-an-hour winds, and tipping tractor trailers across the interstate. Several times I pulled over to wait out alerts of particularly life-threatening super cells, or to unclaw my hands from the steering wheel. What should have been a nine hour drive became fifteen, and I did not make it to Page until late into the night. 

I had booked a hotel as close to the Lake's edge as I could find; a resort that had once advertised itself as *lakeside*, but now disclaimed on their booking portal that their "View rooms may have limited views due to declining water levels." And so I was not expecting, when I threw my open room curtains in the the morning, to see the lake spread before me, turned softly pink and orange by the sunrise. Without stopping to make coffee, I slipped on my shoes and went to the water. 

I had learned five years before, while working along the upper ledges of the Grand Canyon, that this is a landscape which quickly distorts human senses of scale. Things on the Colorado Plateau are always much larger and farther than they seem--but I was still surprised, as I moved toward the Lake, to find it so far away and so low beneath me. I shuffled for fifteen minutes down a road which cut steeply down a sandy slope, until the pavement abruptly ended and emptied onto a beach clumped with Russian Thistles. At the water's edge, the beach was hemmed by four feet or so of wet sand which crumbled easily into the Lake--too easily for me to get close. I watched a small flock of coots rummage in a cove--they seemed to like the pondweed, which protruded up through the water's surface and was tossed around its fringes--but when I moved too quickly, the coots splashed away. Like geese in a city pond, they seemed to enjoy this human-made habitat, although they had not yet grown accustomed to humans themselves.

As I made my way back up to the hotel--which I could see now was more than a hundred feet above me, perched high on a rocky outcrop, I watched the landscape slide through a series of rapid transitions--it was if I was moving between zones on a mountain, but the transitions were much too quick, the zones too slim. Wet sand, then loose sand--with chains--, then crusted sand--some piled high as construction crews rearranged the road--and then landscaped with grey gravel, then red gravel planted with desert plants, then the green watered lawn that abutted the hotel. Scale had been distorted again--but this time, too small. A strata, not of geologic scale but of the last 20 years of drought. I could see where the Lake had been gone long enough for hotel managers to draw up landscaping plans, and where it had receded only so recently that they had not had time to extend the road.
